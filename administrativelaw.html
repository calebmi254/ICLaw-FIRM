Practice: Administrative Law

Promoting Trust in Governance Through Law, Responsibility, and Accountability

At ICL, Administrative Law is recognized not only as a regulatory framework but as a fundamental component of effective public service. It outlines the proper exercise of authority, guides decision-making processes, and safeguards citizens from arbitrary actions. We view administrative law as a vital link between institutional authority and accountability, ensuring that leadership remains responsible to the public.

Administrative Law encompasses the legal principles that regulate the actions, procedures, and decisions of public authorities. Its purpose is to ensure that government agencies:
  • Act within legal boundaries
  • Make decisions that are fair and transparent
  • Are accountable for misuse or abuse of power
  • Provide remedies for individuals affected by unjust administrative conduct
In the context of South Sudan, administrative law plays a crucial role in rebuilding institutional trust and protecting vulnerable populations.

  • Legality: All administrative actions must be supported by law
  • Fairness: Ensuring impartial and respectful treatment of citizens
  • Transparency: Making decision-making processes clear, reasoned, and accessible
  • Right to Be Heard (Audi Alteram Partem)
  • Impartiality (Nemo Judex in Causa Sua)
  • Reasonableness and Proportionality
  • Judicial Review: Allowing individuals to challenge unlawful administrative decisions
These principles serve as procedural safeguards that uphold justice and fairness in administrative processes.

Our work is guided by:
  • The Transitional Constitution of South Sudan, particularly Articles 19, 26, and 54
  • The Administrative Procedures Act (where applicable)
  • The African Charter on Human and Peoples’ Rights
  • International standards related to procedural fairness and good governance
Additionally, we incorporate customary and cultural considerations to ensure administrative justice aligns with community values and societal expectations.

  • Review and Appeal of Government Decisions: Challenging unlawful or unfair administrative actions
  • Drafting Administrative Charters: Developing guiding documents for ministries, agencies, and public bodies
  • Conducting Hearings and Mediation: Facilitating restorative processes for resolving public disputes
  • Training Public Officials: Enhancing understanding of constitutional guidelines and emotional intelligence in decision-making
  • Legal Support for Citizens: Particularly for youth, displaced persons, and marginalized communities

We believe that public administration should be grounded in legality, humility, and emotional intelligence. Public service is a duty rooted in responsibility, not privilege. Citizens deserve transparency, respect, and accessible recourse when administrative actions cause harm.
CSR Initiative

Know Your Constitution Powered by ICL

To empower South Sudanese youth and communities with comprehensive knowledge of their constitutional rights and responsibilities, emphasizing the vital role of law in fostering national unity, dignity, and intergenerational reconciliation.

The Constitution as a Covenant
A foundational agreement between the people and the state; a framework for restraint, justice, and emotionally intelligent leadership.
Rights and Freedoms
Protecting life, dignity, equality, and fundamental freedoms; access to education, healthcare, and justice for all.
Duties of Citizenship
Promote unity, respect the rights of others, and support national heritage and public institutions with integrity.
A. Youth Constitution Circles
Regular meetings in schools and community centers facilitated by legal educators and youth representatives. Topics: “Understanding the Constitution,” “Justice and Restraint,” “Law as a Legacy.”
B. Pledge of Constitutional Guardianship
Formal Commitment
“In commitment to unity, dignity, and remembrance, I pledge to uphold the Constitution of South Sudan—not as a tool of division, but as a foundation of healing. I commit to protecting the rights of all citizens, acting with restraint, and leading my community with honor.”
C. Mobile Constitutional Outreach
Legal education in rural and flood-affected regions, simplified constitutional materials in local languages, and on-site civic ceremonies and mediation.
